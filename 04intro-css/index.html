Know Your Cheese

History of Cheese Making

Cheese making dates back thousands of years, likely originating around 8000 to 7000 BCE, shortly after humans first began domesticating animals like sheep and goats. It's believed that cheese was discovered by accident—milk stored in animal stomachs would have curdled naturally due to the presence of rennet, an enzyme found in the stomach lining, creating early forms of cheese. This process provided a way to preserve milk in a more stable and portable form.

By 3000 BCE, ancient civilizations such as the Mesopotamians, Egyptians, and Greeks were already producing cheese. Their versions were typically salty and acidic, used primarily for nourishment and long-term storage rather than gourmet enjoyment.

The craft of cheese making saw significant refinement during the Roman Empire. The Romans not only improved techniques for curdling, pressing, and aging cheese but also spread these practices throughout Europe as their empire expanded. This helped establish regional styles and traditions that would evolve over centuries.

During the Middle Ages, cheese making became an important monastic tradition. Monks in European monasteries played a major role in developing and perfecting many of the aged cheeses we still know today, such as Roquefort in France, Parmesan in Italy, and various soft-ripened varieties like Brie.

The Renaissance and the early modern period saw cheese becoming more widespread and diverse across Europe. By the 19th century, the rise of microbiology and food science led to better understanding of fermentation and hygiene. This, along with pasteurization, allowed cheese to be produced more consistently and safely.

In the 20th century, cheese making underwent industrialization, particularly in places like the United States, where mass production techniques allowed for large-scale distribution. Despite this shift, traditional cheese making still thrives today, with artisan producers continuing to preserve centuries-old methods. Today, cheese is enjoyed in countless varieties around the world, blending ancient techniques with modern innovation.

How Cheese is Made

Cheese is made through a process that involves coagulating milk, separating the solids (curds) from the liquid (whey), and then aging or processing the curds. Here's a simplified breakdown of the main steps:

  1. Milk Selection

    • Cheese starts with milk — usually from cows, goats, or sheep.
    • The milk is typically pasteurized to kill harmful bacteria, although some cheeses use raw milk.

  2. Acidification

    • A starter culture (good bacteria) is added to the milk.
    • These bacteria convert lactose (milk sugar) into lactic acid, developing flavor and acidity.

  3. Coagulation

    • Rennet, an enzyme, is added to curdle the milk.
    • The milk separates into curds (solids) and whey (liquids).

  4. Cutting the Curds

    • The curds are cut into smaller pieces to release more whey.
    • Smaller curds result in drier cheese.

  5. Cooking & Stirring

    • The curds may be gently heated and stirred.
    • This helps remove more whey and firms up the curds.

  6. Draining & Salting

    • The whey is drained off from the curds.
    • Curds are salted by either mixing in salt or soaking in a brine solution.

  7. Shaping & Pressing

    • Curds are placed into molds and often pressed.
    • This gives the cheese its shape and removes excess whey.

  8. Aging (Affinage)

    • Some cheeses are aged from weeks to years in controlled environments.
    • During aging, flavors develop and textures change.

Variations in milk type, bacteria used, aging time, moisture, add-ons, etc. all contribute to the huge variety of cheeses around the world.

Kinds of Cheese

There are many different kinds of cheese, each with their own flavors, origins, and quirks. Here's a list of cheese among our selection:

Havarti
Origin: Denmark
Fun Fact: Havarti was created in the 19th century by a Danish woman named Hanne Nielsen, who was passionate about modernizing cheese making. It's one of the few cheeses that can be aged or eaten young — aging it gives it a nutty flavor!
Muenster
Origin: United States (inspired by French Munster cheese)
Fun Fact: American Muenster is known for its orange rind, which comes from annatto, a natural food coloring made from seeds. Despite its bold color, Muenster is incredibly mild and melts beautifully, making it a grilled cheese superstar.
Brie
Origin: France
Fun Fact: Often called the "Queen of Cheeses," Brie was reportedly served at the Congress of Vienna in 1815, where it won over diplomats and royalty alike. Its white, edible rind is made of mold — don't worry, it's completely safe and delicious!
Parmesan (Parmigiano-Reggiano)
Origin: Italy
Fun Fact: True Parmigiano-Reggiano must be aged at least 12 months — some wheels are aged over 36 months! It's so valued in Italy that wheels of it have even been used as bank collateral.
Roquefort
Origin: France
Fun Fact: Roquefort is made with sheep's milk and aged in natural limestone caves in the village of Roquefort-sur-Soulzon. Legend has it the mold was discovered when a young shepherd left his lunch in a cave and returned later to find blue-veined cheese.
Cheddar
Origin: England
Fun Fact: The term “Cheddaring” refers to the special process of stacking and turning curds to drain whey — unique to this cheese. The world's largest Cheddar ever made weighed over 56,000 pounds!
Gouda
Origin: Netherlands
Fun Fact: Gouda accounts for over 50% of Dutch cheese production. Older (aged) Gouda becomes caramel-colored, hard, and develops crunchy crystals called tyrosine, which are actually amino acid clusters — and cheese nerds love them.
Mozzarella
Origin: Italy
Fun Fact: Traditional mozzarella is made from water buffalo milk (mozzarella di bufala). It's one of the few cheeses that's stretched — a process called pasta filata — giving it that signature stringy texture when melted.
