Foods and Tools for Cheese

Complimentary Foods

When enjoying cheese, complementary foods help enhance the flavor, texture, and aroma of the cheeses. Here's a list of complementary foods, categorized by type, that pair well with cheese for a balanced and enjoyable experience:

Breads & Crackers

Baguette slices

  • Neutral flavor and a good texture that pairs well with most cheeses.

Water crackers

  • Plain and crisp, allowing the cheese flavor to shine.

Whole grain crackers

  • Adds a nutty flavor that complements sharp and aged cheeses.

Crostini or breadsticks

  • Crunchy texture provides contrast to soft cheeses.

Rye bread or pumpernickel

  • Bold, earthy flavors that pair well with strong cheeses like blue or aged cheddar.

Fruits

Grapes

  • Classic pairing with cheese, offering a sweet and refreshing contrast.

Apples

  • Crisp and slightly acidic; great with cheddar and semi-hard cheeses.

Pears

  • Mild sweetness that pairs beautifully with blue cheese and goat cheese.

Figs

  • Fresh or dried, they offer a luxurious sweetness that enhances creamy or tangy cheeses.

Strawberries and other berries

  • Bright, slightly tart flavors contrast well with creamy or soft cheeses.

Dried apricots

  • Concentrated sweetness balances out salty or sharp cheeses.

Dried cranberries

  • Sweet and tart, good with nutty or tangy cheeses.

Dates

  • Rich sweetness that pairs well with bold cheeses like blue or aged Gouda.

Raisins

  • Classic dried fruit with a sweet flavor that softens the sharpness of cheese.

Nuts

Almonds

  • Especially Marcona almonds — great with Manchego and other firm cheeses.

Walnuts

  • Earthy and slightly bitter; good contrast for blue cheeses.

Pistachios

  • Mild and buttery, pairs well with both soft and hard cheeses.

Pecans

  • Slightly sweet and crunchy; excellent with creamy cheeses like Brie or Camembert.

Meats

Prosciutto

  • Thin, salty cured meat that pairs perfectly with soft cheeses like mozzarella or burrata.

Salami

  • Robust flavor complements firm and aged cheeses.

Soppressata

  • Rich, spiced cured meat ideal with bold cheeses.

Coppa

  • Flavorful and fatty; pairs nicely with nutty or hard cheeses.

Spreads & Condiments

Honey

  • Sweetness that contrasts and balances pungent or salty cheeses like blue cheese.

Fig jam or quince paste (membrillo)

  • Rich, fruity spreads that work well with firm cheeses like Manchego or aged Gouda.

Chutneys

  • Spiced and sweet — mango or apple chutneys go great with sharp or tangy cheeses.

Mustards

  • Grainy or Dijon mustards provide a savory and tangy bite to aged cheeses.

Olive tapenade

  • Salty and umami-rich, complements sharp and creamy cheeses.

Vegetables & Pickled Items

Cornichons

  • Small pickles that provide a tart, crunchy contrast to rich cheeses.

Pickled onions

  • Sharp and tangy; cut through the creaminess of soft cheeses.

Roasted red peppers

  • Sweet and smoky flavor that pairs well with mild or creamy cheeses.

Olives

  • Briny and bold — ideal with hard cheeses like Pecorino or Parmigiano.

Cherry tomatoes

  • Juicy and sweet; excellent with fresh cheeses like mozzarella or burrata.

Chocolate

Dark chocolate

  • Pairs beautifully with bold cheeses like blue cheese or aged Gouda.

Milk chocolate

  • Softens the intensity of cheeses like Brie; offers a sweet contrast.

Special Cheese Tools

It turns out that cheese is such a sensation throughout the world, that special equipment have been made specifically to enhance the experience cheese can provide, be it in cheese making or in preparing unique cheese dishes. Here's a list of a few of these tools:

Fondue Pot (Caquelon)
Cheese: Gruyère, Emmental, Comté (for Swiss-style cheese fondue)
Purpose: To melt cheese and keep it warm for dipping bread, vegetables, etc.
Special features: Ceramic or cast iron pot, often with a burner or tea light underneath.
Raclette Melter
Cheese: Raclette
Purpose: Melts the surface of the cheese wheel or half-wheel, which is then scraped onto potatoes, bread, or meats.
Variants: Tabletop electric models or large professional infrared models.
Girolle
Cheese: Tête de Moine
Purpose: Scrapes thin rosettes from the top of the cheese to enhance aroma and flavor.
Design: A vertical rod pierces the cheese, and a blade scrapes circularly.
Cheese Harp (Curd Cutter)
Cheese: Used in cheesemaking for all types of cheese curds.
Purpose: Cuts curds evenly after the milk is coagulated.
Used by: Artisan and industrial cheesemakers.
Cheese Cave or Humidity-Controlled Fridge
Cheese: Blue cheese, bloomy rind cheeses (Brie, Camembert), washed-rind cheeses.
Purpose: Aging and storing cheese under precise humidity and temperature.
Cheese Wire / Wire Cutter
Cheese: Soft cheeses or blues like Roquefort, Stilton, Gorgonzola.
Purpose: Clean, non-stick cuts without crushing the interior.
Provoleta Pan (Argentina)
Cheese: Provoleta (grilled provolone)
Purpose: Cast iron or ceramic dish to cook the cheese directly on the grill or stovetop until bubbling and crisp on the edges.
Mozzarella Stretching Trough
Cheese: Mozzarella, Burrata
Purpose: Used in cheesemaking to stretch curd in hot water or whey.
Usually found in: Professional cheese kitchens.
Cheese Safe / Cloche
Cheese: Soft-rind or bloomy cheeses served at room temp.
Purpose: Allows cheeses to breathe while protecting from pests and preserving aroma.
Parmigiano Reggiano Breaker (Almond Knife)
Cheese: Parmigiano Reggiano, Grana Padano
Purpose: Splits the hard cheese into rough chunks rather than slicing it.
Fun fact: This is the traditional way to open a whole wheel.
